\begin{abstract}
We apply the temporal asymmetry parameter $\tauasym = 1 - F$ from the
Petz recovery framework [S.-K.\ Huang, companion paper] to the
gravitational dephasing channel of Pikovski et~al., obtaining an
explicit, time-dependent quantification of ``collapse'' as Petz
recovery failure. For a system with $N$ internal degrees of freedom,
$\tauasym(t) = 1 - \exp(-N\Gamma t^2)$ and the quantum Darwinism
redundancy $R(t) = N[1 - \exp(-\Gamma t^2)]$ are governed by the same
parameter $N\Gamma$, establishing a \emph{$\tauasym$-$R$ bridge}:
irreversibility ($\tauasym \to 1$) and objectivity ($R \to N$) emerge
at the same timescale. Under the Diósi--Penrose assumption, the
collapse timescale reduces to $t_c = \hbar/E_G$. We connect
$\tauasym = 1$ to operational superselection via the
Englert--Greenberger wave-particle duality relation and spectrum
broadcast structure, providing a quantitative language for collapse within standard
quantum mechanics, with explicit bounds on spatial recoverability.
Predictions for molecule
interferometry, optomechanical, and gravitational entanglement
experiments are tabulated and shown to be consistent with all
existing data. The universal bound
$F_{\mathrm{spatial}}(t) \geq \exp(-\Sigma_{\mathrm{grav}}(t)/2)$
constrains the recoverability of spatial coherence for any
gravitational channel.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%-----------------------------------------------------------------------

In a companion paper~\cite{Paper1}, we established that the Petz
recovery map~\cite{Petz1986,Petz1988} connects quantum erasure,
Bayesian retrodiction, quantum error correction, and thermodynamic
irreversibility. The temporal asymmetry parameter
\begin{equation}
\tauasym = 1 - F\!\big(\rho,\,
  \tilde{\Petz}_{\sigma,\chan}(\chan(\rho))\big)
\label{eq:tau_def}
\end{equation}
quantifies the degree to which a quantum channel $\chan$ distinguishes
past from future: $\tauasym = 0$ for closed (unitary) evolution,
$\tauasym > 0$ for open systems, with the bound
$\tauasym \leq 1 - \exp(-\Sigma/2)$ linking recovery failure to
entropy production~$\Sigma$~\cite{Junge2018,Fawzi2015}.

A central question in quantum foundations remains: when does a quantum
superposition become a classical mixture?  The decoherence
program~\cite{Zurek2003,Joos1985,Schlosshauer2007} identifies
environment-induced decoherence as the mechanism, but leaves open
what distinguishes ``for all practical purposes'' (FAPP) collapse
from genuine irreversibility. Meanwhile, Penrose~\cite{Penrose1996}
and Di\'{o}si~\cite{Diosi1987} have argued that gravitational
self-energy sets a fundamental collapse timescale $t_c = \hbar/E_G$.

In this Letter, we show that the $\tauasym$ framework provides a
precise, quantitative answer.  We define \emph{operational collapse}
as $\tauasym \to 1$: the Petz recovery map---the unique Bayesian
retrodiction functor~\cite{Parzygnat2023}---fails completely,
certifying that no physical protocol can recover the prior
superposition. We derive $\tauasym(t)$ explicitly for the
gravitational dephasing channel of Pikovski et~al.~\cite{Pikovski2015}
[Sec.~\ref{sec:channel}], connect internal and spatial decoherence
via an entanglement complementarity bridge [Sec.~\ref{sec:bridge}],
establish a quantitative link between irreversibility and objectivity
(the $\tauasym$-$R$ bridge) [Sec.~\ref{sec:tau_R}], recover the
Penrose timescale under the Di\'{o}si--Penrose assumption
[Sec.~\ref{sec:penrose}], and tabulate predictions for current and
proposed experiments [Sec.~\ref{sec:experiments}].  Each result is
labeled [\textsc{known}], [\textsc{new synthesis}], or
[\textsc{new formula}].

\section{Gravitational Dephasing as a Quantum Channel}
\label{sec:channel}
%-----------------------------------------------------------------------

\emph{Pikovski Hamiltonian}~[\textsc{known}].---Consider a massive
particle in a spatial superposition
$\ket{\Psi(0)} = (\alpha\ket{x_1} + \beta\ket{x_2}) \otimes
\ket{\chi_{\mathrm{int}}}$, where $\ket{\chi_{\mathrm{int}}}$ is the
internal state.  In the post-Newtonian approximation, the total
Hamiltonian is~\cite{Pikovski2015}
\begin{equation}
H = \frac{p^2}{2m} + H_{\mathrm{int}}
  \!\left(1 + \frac{\Phi(\hat{x})}{c^2}\right),
\label{eq:pikovski_H}
\end{equation}
where $\Phi(\hat{x})$ is the Newtonian gravitational potential
evaluated at the center-of-mass position.  Time evolution under
Eq.~\eqref{eq:pikovski_H} produces~\cite{Pikovski2015}
\begin{equation}
\ket{\Psi(t)} = \alpha\ket{x_1}\otimes U_1\ket{\chi}
  + \beta\ket{x_2}\otimes U_2\ket{\chi},
\label{eq:psi_t}
\end{equation}
where $U_j = \exp(-i H_{\mathrm{int}}(1 + \Phi_j/c^2)\,t/\hbar)$
with $\Phi_j \equiv \Phi(x_j)$.

\emph{Decoherence factor}~[\textsc{known}].---Tracing over internal
degrees of freedom yields the reduced spatial state
\begin{equation}
\rho_S(t) = \begin{pmatrix}
|\alpha|^2 & \alpha\beta^* D(t) \\
\alpha^*\beta\, D^*(t) & |\beta|^2
\end{pmatrix},
\label{eq:rho_S}
\end{equation}
with decoherence factor
\begin{equation}
D(t) = \bra{\chi}\exp\!\left(\frac{i H_{\mathrm{int}}
  \Delta\Phi\, t}{\hbar c^2}\right)\ket{\chi}
= \sum_n |c_n|^2 e^{-i E_n \Delta\Phi\, t/(\hbar c^2)},
\label{eq:D_factor}
\end{equation}
where $\Delta\Phi = \Phi(x_1) - \Phi(x_2)$, $\{E_n\}$ are internal
energy eigenvalues, and $c_n = \braket{E_n}{\chi}$.

\emph{CPTP dephasing channel}~[\textsc{known}].---The map
$\chan_{\mathrm{grav}}: \rho_S(0) \mapsto \rho_S(t)$ is a
completely positive, trace-preserving (CPTP) dephasing channel with
Kraus operators~\cite{Schlosshauer2007}
\begin{equation}
K_0 = \sqrt{\frac{1+p}{2}}\, I, \qquad
K_1 = \sqrt{\frac{1-p}{2}}\, \sigma_z,
\label{eq:kraus}
\end{equation}
where $p(t) = |D(t)|$ and $\sigma_z = \op{x_1}{x_1} - \op{x_2}{x_2}$.
One verifies $K_0^\dagger K_0 + K_1^\dagger K_1 = I$ and
$\chan_{\mathrm{grav}}(\rho)_{12} = p(t)\,\rho_{12}$.

\emph{Many internal modes}~[\textsc{known}].---For $N$ internal modes
in a thermal state at temperature $T$, the decoherence factor
factorizes:
$D(t) = \prod_{k=1}^{N} d_k(t)$, where each mode contributes
$d_k(t) = 1 - \varepsilon_k(t)$ with
$\varepsilon_k \ll 1$.  The product yields
\begin{equation}
|D(t)| \approx \exp\!\left(-\sum_k \varepsilon_k\right)
= \exp(-N\langle\varepsilon\rangle),
\label{eq:D_product}
\end{equation}
giving Gaussian decay $p(t) = \exp(-\Gamma t^2)$ with
$\Gamma = (\Delta E)^2 (\Delta\Phi)^2/(2\hbar^2 c^4)$ for a thermal
internal state with energy variance~$(\Delta E)^2$~\cite{Pikovski2015}.

\emph{Temporal asymmetry parameter}~[\textsc{new synthesis}].---The
Petz recovery map for the dephasing channel with maximally mixed
reference state is self-adjoint: $\Petz = \chan_{\mathrm{grav}}$.
Hence the recovery fidelity (worst case, $\ket{+}$ input) is
$F_{\mathrm{Petz}} = (1 + p^2)/2$.  Using the universal (rotated)
Petz map~\cite{Junge2018} and defining
$\tauasym(t) = 1 - F(t)$, we obtain
\begin{equation}
\boxed{
\tauasym_{\mathrm{grav}}(t)
= 1 - \exp\!\big(-N\Gamma t^2\big)
}
\label{eq:tau_grav}
\end{equation}
for $N$ internal modes in the large-$N$ limit.  This is a monotone
from $\tauasym = 0$ (full coherence) to $\tauasym \to 1$
(operational collapse), with characteristic timescale
\begin{equation}
t_{\mathrm{dec}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{N\Gamma}}
= \frac{\hbar c^2 \sqrt{2}}{\sqrt{N}\,\Delta E\,|\Delta\Phi|}.
\label{eq:t_dec}
\end{equation}

\emph{Entropy production}~[\textsc{known}].---The entropy production
of the gravitational dephasing channel is
$\Sigma(t) = H_{\mathrm{bin}}((1+p(t))/2)$, where
$H_{\mathrm{bin}}(x) = -x\log x - (1-x)\log(1-x)$ is the binary
entropy.  The bound~$\tauasym \leq 1 - \exp(-\Sigma/2)$
from Ref.~\cite{Paper1} is satisfied identically.

\section{Internal--Spatial Bridge}
\label{sec:bridge}
%-----------------------------------------------------------------------

[\textsc{new synthesis}]  The state~\eqref{eq:psi_t} is a pure
bipartite state on $\hilb_{\mathrm{spatial}} \otimes
\hilb_{\mathrm{internal}}$.  Partial traces define two dephasing
channels: $\chan_S = \Tr_{\mathrm{int}}$ (yielding $\rho_S$) and
$\chan_I = \Tr_{\mathrm{spatial}}$ (yielding $\rho_I$).  Define
$\tauasym_S$ and $\tauasym_I$ as the temporal asymmetry parameters for
each channel, evaluated for the worst-case input (equal
superposition).

For the spatial channel, $\tauasym_S = (1 - |D(t)|)/2$.  For the
internal channel, $\tauasym_I = (1 - |D(t)|^2)/2$.  Eliminating
$|D|$ yields the \emph{bridge relation}:
\begin{equation}
\boxed{
\tauasym_I = 2\,\tauasym_S\,(1 - \tauasym_S).
}
\label{eq:bridge}
\end{equation}
This is a concave parabola with maximum $\tauasym_I = 1/2$
at $\tauasym_S = 1/2$, reflecting the fact that a qubit
dephasing channel saturates at $\tauasym = 1/2$.

\emph{Entanglement entropy}~[\textsc{known}].---Since the total state
is pure, the von~Neumann entropy of either subsystem equals the
entanglement entropy:
\begin{equation}
S_{\mathrm{ent}}(t) = H_{\mathrm{bin}}\!\big(1 - \tauasym_S(t)\big).
\label{eq:S_ent}
\end{equation}
The entropy production of both channels equals the entanglement
entropy:
\begin{equation}
\Sigma_{\mathrm{int}} = \Sigma_{\mathrm{spatial}}
= S_{\mathrm{ent}}.
\label{eq:sigma_ent}
\end{equation}
This identity~[\textsc{new synthesis}] follows because both channels
arise from tracing out the complementary subsystem of a pure state,
so the DPI decrease $\Delta D$ equals $S_{\mathrm{ent}}$ in both
cases.

\emph{Englert--Greenberger complementarity}~[\textsc{new
synthesis}].---The wave-particle duality relation of Englert and
Greenberger~\cite{Englert1996,Greenberger1988} takes the form
$\mathcal{V}^2 + \mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{wp}}^2 \leq 1$, where
$\mathcal{V}$ is visibility and $\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{wp}}$ is
which-path distinguishability.  In the $\tauasym$ language:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{V} = 1 - 2\tauasym_S, \qquad
\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{wp}} = \sqrt{2\tauasym_I}.
\label{eq:EG_tau}
\end{equation}
The duality relation becomes
$\mathcal{V}^2 + \mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{wp}}^2
= (1 - 2\tauasym_S)^2 + 2\tauasym_I = 1$, which follows identically
from Eq.~\eqref{eq:bridge}.  When $\tauasym_I = 1/2$ (the internal
degrees of freedom carry complete which-path information),
$\mathcal{V} = 0$: spatial coherence vanishes.  This is operationally
indistinguishable from ``collapse'' into a position eigenstate.

\emph{Pointer basis from the equivalence principle}~[\textsc{new
synthesis}].---The equivalence principle dictates that the
gravitational potential $\Phi(\hat{x})$ in
Eq.~\eqref{eq:pikovski_H} couples to \emph{position}:
the internal Hamiltonian acquires a position-dependent
correction $H_{\mathrm{int}} \cdot \Phi(\hat{x})/c^2$.
By Zurek's einselection criterion~\cite{Zurek2003}, the
interaction Hamiltonian selects the pointer basis as the
eigenbasis of the system observable that commutes with the
coupling.  Since $\Phi(\hat{x})$ is diagonal in position,
gravity universally selects \emph{position} as the pointer
basis.  Unlike electromagnetic or nuclear decoherence, where
the pointer basis depends on the details of the specific
interaction, gravitational einselection is universal---it
applies to any massive system regardless of its internal
composition.  This explains why macroscopic objects always
have definite positions (not definite momenta or energy
superpositions): the equivalence principle singles out
position as the only gravitationally robust observable.

\section{From Decoherence to Objectivity: The $\tauasym$-$R$ Bridge}
\label{sec:tau_R}
%-----------------------------------------------------------------------

The previous sections established that $\tauasym \to 1$ certifies
irreversibility.  We now show that $\tauasym \to 1$ and objectivity
(in the sense of quantum Darwinism~\cite{Zurek2009}) emerge from the
\emph{same} physical parameter.

\emph{Tensor product amplification}~[\textsc{known}].---The
decoherence factor~\eqref{eq:D_product} is a product over $N$
independent modes.  Each mode records which-path information
$\varepsilon_k \ll 1$, but the product decays exponentially:
$|D| \approx \exp(-N\langle\varepsilon\rangle)$.  This
exponential-in-$N$ suppression is a direct consequence of the tensor
product structure of quantum mechanics (an axiom, not derived from
dynamics)~\cite{Joos1985,Schlosshauer2007}.  Even for
$\langle\varepsilon\rangle \sim 10^{-20}$, the factor $N \sim
10^{23}$ of a macroscopic object drives
$|D| \to 0$ and $\tauasym \to 1$.

\emph{Quantum Darwinism redundancy}~[\textsc{known}].---In the
framework of quantum Darwinism~\cite{Zurek2009}, the redundancy $R$
counts the number of independent environmental fragments that each
contain sufficient information to determine the system's state.  For
the gravitational dephasing channel with $N$ internal modes, each
mode acquires which-path information at rate $\Gamma_{\mathrm{single}}
= \Gamma/N$.  A fragment of size $f\cdot N$ modes gives partial
decoherence factor $|D_f| = \exp(-fN\Gamma_{\mathrm{single}}t^2) =
\exp(-f\Gamma t^2)$, which becomes negligible when
$f > f_c = 1/(\Gamma t^2)$.  The redundancy
is~\cite{Zurek2009,Riedel2017}
\begin{equation}
R(t) = \frac{1}{f_c} = N\!\left[1 - \exp(-\Gamma_{\mathrm{single}}
  t^2)\right].
\label{eq:R_darwin}
\end{equation}

\emph{The $\tauasym$-$R$ bridge}~[\textsc{new formula}].---Comparing
Eqs.~\eqref{eq:tau_grav} and~\eqref{eq:R_darwin}, both are governed
by the same dephasing parameter $\Gamma_{\mathrm{single}}$:
\begin{equation}
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
\tauasym(t) &= 1 - \exp\!\big(-N\Gamma_{\mathrm{single}} t^2\big), \\
R(t) &= N\!\left[1 - \exp(-\Gamma_{\mathrm{single}} t^2)\right].
\end{aligned}
}
\label{eq:tau_R_bridge}
\end{equation}
At the decoherence time $t_{\mathrm{dec}} = 1/\sqrt{N
\Gamma_{\mathrm{single}}}$, we have $\tauasym(t_{\mathrm{dec}})
= 1 - 1/e \approx 0.63$ and
$R(t_{\mathrm{dec}}) \approx N(1 - e^{-1/N}) \approx 1$ for
$N \gg 1$.  At time $t = \sqrt{\ln N/\Gamma_{\mathrm{single}}}$,
$\tauasym \to 1 - N^{-N} \approx 1$ and $R \to N(1 - 1/N) \approx N$.
This demonstrates that \emph{collapse ($\tauasym \to 1$) and
objectivity ($R \to N$) occur at the same timescale}---they are
two manifestations of the same exponential-in-$N$ decoherence
process.

\emph{Spectrum broadcast structure (SBS)}~[\textsc{new
synthesis}].---Korbicz et~al.~\cite{Korbicz2021} proved that any
von~Neumann-type interaction with $N \gg 1$ sub-environments
generically produces spectrum broadcast structure:
\begin{equation}
\rho_{SE}(t) = \sum_i p_i\, \op{i}{i}_S \otimes
  \rho^{(1)}_i \otimes \rho^{(2)}_i \otimes \cdots \otimes
  \rho^{(N)}_i,
\label{eq:SBS}
\end{equation}
where $\{\ket{i}\}$ are the pointer states and $\rho^{(k)}_i$ are
distinguishable states for each environmental fragment.  The
gravitational dephasing interaction~\eqref{eq:pikovski_H} is a
von~Neumann measurement of position by internal degrees of freedom:
$H_{\mathrm{int}} = \sum_k E_k \op{E_k}{E_k}$ coupled to
$\Phi(\hat{x})$.  By the Korbicz theorem, this produces SBS with
pointer states $\{\ket{x_1}, \ket{x_2}\}$.  SBS is the strongest
known condition for ``objective existence''~\cite{Korbicz2021}: it
ensures (i) the system is in a definite pointer state, (ii) multiple
observers independently agree on the outcome, and (iii) observation
does not disturb the state.

\emph{Why gravity is special}~[\textsc{new synthesis}].---Among all
interactions that produce decoherence, gravity occupies a unique
position.  By the equivalence principle, gravity couples universally
to all forms of energy-momentum: no form of matter or radiation is
gravitationally neutral~\cite{Penrose1996}.  Furthermore, gravity
cannot be shielded---there is no gravitational analogue of a Faraday
cage~\cite{Pikovski2015}.  Every other source of decoherence
(electromagnetic, nuclear, thermal) can, in principle, be
eliminated by sufficient isolation.  Gravitational dephasing
cannot.  Consequently, $\tauasym_{\mathrm{grav}}$ constitutes
the \emph{irreducible floor} of temporal asymmetry: for any
massive system in a spatial superposition,
$\tauasym_{\mathrm{total}} \geq \tauasym_{\mathrm{grav}} > 0$.
This is the fundamental lower bound on collapse for massive
objects, independent of engineering improvements in isolation.
The universality of gravity (coupling to all
energy-momentum) together with its unshieldability makes
gravitational decoherence qualitatively different from all other
decoherence mechanisms---it is the only source that is both
universal and inescapable~\cite{Penrose1996,Pikovski2015,DSW2022}.

\emph{$\tauasym = 1$ and operational superselection}~[\textsc{new
synthesis}].---When $\tauasym = 1$, no recovery map
$\Petz$ exists such that $\Petz \circ \chan \approx \id$.  This
means no observable in the accessible algebra (spatial observables
after tracing out internal degrees of freedom) can distinguish
$\rho_S$ from the decohered state
$\rho_S^{\mathrm{dec}} = |\alpha|^2 \op{x_1}{x_1} + |\beta|^2
\op{x_2}{x_2}$.  This is operationally equivalent to a
\emph{superselection rule} on position~\cite{BRS2007}: the
coherent superposition is operationally meaningless, and the system
behaves as if in one definite position.

For gravitational decoherence specifically, the connection to
superselection is strengthened by two results:
(i)~Danielson, Satishchandran, and Wald~\cite{DSW2022} showed
that superpositions of masses radiate soft gravitons that cross the
cosmological horizon---information is causally irretrievable, not
merely practically inaccessible.
(ii)~Kiefer and Joos~\cite{KieferJoos1999} proved that with
infinitely many graviton field modes, the decoherence is permanent
(no Poincar\'{e} recurrence).
Together with the infrared superselection
structure~\cite{Buchholz1986}, $\tauasym = 1$ from gravitational
dephasing may be exact rather than approximate, providing genuine
(not FAPP) irreversibility within standard quantum mechanics.

\section{Connection to the Penrose Timescale}
\label{sec:penrose}
%-----------------------------------------------------------------------

\emph{The Di\'{o}si--Penrose assumption}~[\textsc{known}].---Di\'{o}si
and Penrose independently proposed that the gravitational self-energy
of a spatial superposition,
\begin{equation}
E_G = G \!\int\! d^3\mathbf{x}\, d^3\mathbf{x}'\;
\frac{[\rho_1(\mathbf{x}) - \rho_2(\mathbf{x})]
      [\rho_1(\mathbf{x}') - \rho_2(\mathbf{x}')]}{|\mathbf{x}
      - \mathbf{x}'|},
\label{eq:E_G}
\end{equation}
where $\rho_1, \rho_2$ are mass distributions corresponding to
$\ket{x_1}, \ket{x_2}$, sets a fundamental collapse rate
$\Lambda = E_G/\hbar$~\cite{Diosi1987,Penrose1996}.

\emph{Penrose timescale from $\tauasym$}~[\textsc{new
synthesis}].---Under the Di\'{o}si--Penrose assumption, the
decoherence factor takes exponential form:
$|D(t)| = \exp(-E_G t/\hbar)$.  Substituting into
Eq.~\eqref{eq:tau_grav} (with the identification
$N\Gamma t^2 \to E_G t/\hbar$ for the Di\'{o}si--Penrose
mechanism), the collapse criterion
$\tauasym(t_c) = 1 - 1/e$ gives
\begin{equation}
t_c = \frac{\hbar}{E_G}\,,
\label{eq:t_penrose}
\end{equation}
reproducing the Penrose timescale.  For a sphere of mass $m$ and
radius $R$ displaced by $\Delta x \gg R$, $E_G \approx Gm^2/R$,
giving $t_c \approx \hbar R/(Gm^2)$.  For a $10^{-14}$~kg object
($R \sim 10\;\mu$m), $t_c \sim 15$~s; for a
$2 \times 10^{-4}$~kg mirror ($R \sim 1$~mm),
$t_c \sim 1.5\;\mu$s~\cite{Penrose1996}.

\emph{Honest limitations}~[\textsc{known}].---Three caveats are
essential:

(i)~The Pikovski mechanism~\cite{Pikovski2015} and the
Di\'{o}si--Penrose mechanism~\cite{Diosi1987,Penrose1996} are
\emph{different physical processes}.  Pikovski dephasing arises from
gravitational time dilation coupling to internal degrees of freedom;
the Di\'{o}si--Penrose mechanism invokes the gravitational self-energy
of the superposition itself.  They coincide only when the internal
energy variance satisfies
$\Delta E \cdot |\Delta\Phi|/(\sqrt{2}\,c^2) = E_G$.  Our
$\tauasym$ framework accommodates both as inputs but does not derive
$E_G$ from first principles.

(ii)~$E_G$ is an input parameter, not an output.  The $\tauasym$
framework provides the operational definition of collapse
($\tauasym \to 1$) and the thermodynamic bound
($F \geq \exp(-\Sigma/2)$), but it does not predict the decoherence
rate; it takes the rate as given and quantifies its consequences.

(iii)~Galley, Giacomini, and Selby~\cite{GGS2023} proved that any
consistent coupling between classical gravity and quantum matter
is \emph{fundamentally irreversible}---no Stinespring dilation
exists.  If gravity has classical aspects, then $\tauasym > 0$ from
gravitational dephasing is genuine (not FAPP), because the channel
has no unitary extension.  If gravity is fully quantum, the total
system admits a unitary dilation, and $\tauasym > 0$ is FAPP---but
operationally indistinguishable in all current experiments.

\emph{$\tauasym$-$R$ bridge prediction}~[\textsc{new formula}].---The
bridge~\eqref{eq:tau_R_bridge} predicts that collapse
($\tauasym \to 1$) and objectivity ($R \to N$) emerge at the same
timescale.  This is testable via quantum Darwinism
experiments~\cite{Unden2019,Zhu2025}: by monitoring both the
decoherence of the system and the buildup of redundant information
in environmental fragments, one can verify that $\tauasym$ and $R$
track the same underlying parameter $N\Gamma_{\mathrm{single}}$.

\emph{BMV experiment}~[\textsc{known}].---The
Bose--Marletto--Vedral experiment~\cite{Bose2017,Marletto2017}
proposes to detect gravitationally-induced entanglement between
two masses.  In our framework, this corresponds to the regime
$\tauasym_{\mathrm{grav}} \sim 0.004$ (for $t = 1$~s,
$m = 10^{-14}$~kg), where partial decoherence is measurable but
collapse is far from complete.  A positive result
(entanglement detected) would demonstrate that $\tauasym < 1$
for the gravitational channel and that gravity mediates quantum
correlations.  A negative result would be consistent with
$\tauasym > 0$ being genuine
(Galley--Giacomini--Selby~\cite{GGS2023}).

\emph{Consistency with existing data}.---All current experiments
satisfy $\tauasym_{\mathrm{grav}} \ll 1$, and observed decoherence
rates are orders of magnitude larger than the gravitational
prediction.  The parameter-free Di\'{o}si model is ruled out by
underground experiments~\cite{Donadi2021}, but Penrose's version
(with nuclear-scale smearing) remains viable.  No experiment
contradicts the bound~\eqref{eq:F_bound}.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
%-----------------------------------------------------------------------

We have shown that wavefunction collapse---the transition from quantum
superposition to classical definiteness---admits a precise
information-theoretic characterization: \emph{operational collapse
is Petz recovery failure} ($\tauasym \to 1$).  The Petz recovery
map, being the unique Bayesian retrodiction
functor~\cite{Parzygnat2023}, certifies that no physical protocol
can infer the prior superposition from the decohered state.

\emph{What we answer.}---Three questions receive quantitative
answers within this framework:

(i)~\emph{Why does asymmetry cause collapse?}  The tensor product
structure of quantum mechanics amplifies any
$\varepsilon_k > 0$ per environmental mode into exponential-in-$N$
suppression of coherence~\cite{Joos1985}.  This is not an
approximation but a mathematical consequence of the Hilbert space
axiom.

(ii)~\emph{Why operationally only one outcome?}  When
$\tauasym = 1$, no observable in the accessible algebra distinguishes
the state from a classical mixture.  Combined with spectrum broadcast
structure (SBS)~\cite{Korbicz2021}, this ensures that all observers
independently agree on one outcome---the operational definition of
objectivity.

(iii)~\emph{What is the timescale?}  The $\tauasym$-$R$
bridge~\eqref{eq:tau_R_bridge} shows that collapse and objectivity
emerge at $t_{\mathrm{dec}} = 1/\sqrt{N\Gamma_{\mathrm{single}}}$.
Under the Di\'{o}si--Penrose assumption, this reduces to the Penrose
timescale $t_c = \hbar/E_G$.

\emph{What we do not answer.}---The \emph{ontological} question
(whether other branches ``cease to exist'') lies outside the
framework.  Our definition of collapse is operational: it concerns
what can be physically measured and recovered, not what
``truly exists.''  The distinction between operational and
ontological collapse is a choice of interpretation (Everett,
Copenhagen, etc.), not a question that $\tauasym$ can settle.
We note, however, that the operational answer is the strongest
statement physics can make without additional metaphysical
commitments.

\emph{Open questions and limitations.}---We identify five
limitations of the present framework that deserve explicit
acknowledgment.

(i)~\emph{Born rule.}  The framework assumes the Born rule in
computing both $F$ (fidelity) and $\Sigma$ (entropy production).
A complete theory of collapse should derive the Born rule from
more primitive axioms.  We do not attempt this; our results are
conditional on standard quantum probability.

(ii)~\emph{Intermediate regime.}  The physics of
$0 < \tauasym < 1$ (partial collapse) deserves further
investigation.  In this regime the system is neither fully
quantum ($\tauasym = 0$) nor fully classical ($\tauasym = 1$).
What operational statements can be made about a system with,
say, $\tauasym = 0.3$?  The bound
$F \geq \exp(-\Sigma/2)$ constrains recovery, but a richer
operational characterization of intermediate $\tauasym$ remains
an open problem.

(iii)~\emph{Observer dependence.}  Different observers---defined
by different system-environment partitions---assign different
$\tauasym$ to the same process.  In a Wigner's friend scenario,
Wigner assigns $\tauasym = 0$ to a process for which Wigner's
friend assigns $\tauasym = 1$.  The framework is internally
consistent ($\tauasym$ is partition-dependent by construction),
but the relationship between partition-dependent $\tauasym$
values and intersubjective agreement requires further analysis.

(iv)~\emph{Separating gravitational decoherence.}  In all
current experiments, non-gravitational decoherence dominates by
many orders of magnitude (Table~\ref{tab:experiments}).  Isolating
the gravitational contribution $\tauasym_{\mathrm{grav}}$
requires either extremely massive quantum superpositions
(e.g., the BMV proposal~\cite{Bose2017}) or space-based experiments
with extraordinary isolation from electromagnetic and thermal
noise.  Until $\tauasym_{\mathrm{grav}}$ is directly measured,
the gravitational predictions of this framework remain untested.

(v)~\emph{Scope of claims.}  This framework provides a
quantitative language for collapse ($\tauasym$) and a universal
bound ($F \geq \exp(-\Sigma/2)$), not a resolution of the
measurement problem.  The measurement problem asks ``why one
outcome?''; we answer ``when recovery becomes impossible.''
These are related but distinct questions: the former is
ontological, the latter operational.

\emph{Relationship to Paper~1.}---This work specializes the general
$\tauasym$ framework of Ref.~\cite{Paper1} to gravitational
channels, demonstrating that the quantum eraser--retrodiction--QEC--thermodynamics
framework extends naturally to the collapse problem.  The
equivalence chain
$\tauasym = 0 \Leftrightarrow \Sigma = 0 \Leftrightarrow
I(A;E|B) = 0 \Leftrightarrow \text{QMC}$~\cite{Paper1} applies
directly: gravitational decoherence breaks all four conditions
simultaneously.

\emph{Outlook.}---The $\tauasym$-$R$ bridge suggests a broader
program.  In a companion paper~\cite{Paper2}, we apply the same
framework to strong gravitational fields, where $\tauasym = r_s/r$
and the Petz recovery bound constrains the structure of spacetime
near horizons.  The connection between decoherence and objectivity
established here may also inform quantum error correction strategies:
quantum error correction can be understood as the deliberate
construction of a local environment where
$\tauasym \approx 0$---an artificial suppression of the arrow of
time.
